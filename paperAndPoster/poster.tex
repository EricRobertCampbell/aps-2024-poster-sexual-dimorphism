\block{Abstract}{While sexual dimorphism is common in extant taxa, detection in extinct ones is made challenging by difficulties in determining the sex and size of fossil organisms combined with the low statistical power of existing methods. Herein is outlined a modification to the method from \cite{saittaEffectSizeStatistical2020} employing Bayesian regression instead of standard non-linear regression to construct sex-specific growth curves for quantifying sexual size dimorphism in dinosaurs.
% which constructs sex-specific growth curves to determine sexual size dimorphism, but using Bayesian regression to construct the curves rather than standard non-linear regression.
 The method is shown to be robust and reliable on both simulated and actual populations, with the additional benefit of incorporating prior information to constrain dimorphism to biologically plausible levels along with the incorporation of uncertainty to give a fuller understanding of the strength of the results.} % See Section 4.2
\begin{columns} % See Section 4.4
\column{0.333333333} % See Section 4.4
\block{Introduction}{
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Sexual dimorphism is common in extant taxa, but positive examples in dinosaurs are rare
    \item Levels of dimorphism can correlate with  ecological behaviours: monogamy, group size, inter- and intrasexual competition, \&c., potentially providing insight into dinosaur ecologies and social structure
    \item Determining sex and size of an extinct organism is difficult, and existing methods to determine dimorphism (Hartigans' dip test, $t$ tests) suffer from low statistical power and confounding by e.g. presence of juveniles
    \item Method in \cite{saittaEffectSizeStatistical2020}: construct growth curves for predicted male and females separately to measure dimorphism. This method can suffer from lack of numerical convergence and implausible predictions (e.g. negative masses), particularly for small sample sizes
    \item Proposed modification: use Bayesian regression to estimate growth curves, getting as a result a posterior distribution for level of dimorphism rather than a point estimate or range
  \end{itemize}
}
\block[bodyinnersep=0mm]{Method}{
  \begin{enumerate}
        \item Gather data: age (or a correlate) and character of interest (e.g. length)
        \item Fit growth curve to population as a whole 
        \item Predict sex by assigning all samples above the population curve to one sex and all below to the other. 
        \item Create a posterior distribution for sex-specific parameters (Bayesian regression) (vs. fit separate growth curves to each predicted sex)
        \item Calculate posterior distribution for dimorphism (vs. calculate maximum likelihood \& confidence interval for dimorphism)
  \end{enumerate}

\block{Verification}{
  Bayes' Theorem:
  \[
    \overbrace{P\left(\text{parameter value}|\text{data}\right)}^{\text{Posterior}} \propto \overbrace{P\left( \text{parameter value} \right)}^{\text{Prior}} \ast \overbrace{P \left( \text{data} | \text{parameter value}\right)}^{\text{Likelihood}}
  \]

    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\colwidth}
      \begin{tikzfigure}[Bayesian parameter estimation]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{images/bayesianExample-poster.png}%
        \label{fig:bayesianExplanation}
      \end{tikzfigure}%
    \end{minipage}%
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\colwidth}
      \begin{itemize}
          \item To estimate mean human height, start with what we know about the height before seeing any data --- the \emph{prior}.
          \item A sample of heights is collected and the \emph{likelihood} of that sample for each of the potential mean heights is calculated.
          \item Combination of those, our final belief about the value of the mean height, is the \emph{posterior} distribution.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{minipage}%
  \begin{itemize}
    \item American alligator (\species{Alligator mississippiensis}) growth parameters from \cite{wilkinsonGrowthRatesAmerican1997} (von Bertalanffy growth curve), but with constant standard deviation


    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\colwidth}
    \section*{Simulated}
    \begin{align*}
    \text{Length}_{ m / f } &\sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu_{ m / f }, \sigma_{m/f}\right) \\
    \mu_m &= 3.79 * \left(1 - 0.94e^{-0.0695 \ast \text{age}}\right) \\
    \mu_f &= 2.78 * \left(1 - 0.91e^{-0.0926 \ast \text{age}}\right) \\
    \sigma_{m/f} &= 0.25
    \end{align*}
  \end{minipage}
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\colwidth}
    \section*{Model}
    \begin{align*}
    \text{Length}_{m/f,i} &\sim \normal{\mu_{m/f, i}}{\sigma_{m / f}} \\
    \mu_{m/f, i} &= \text{von Bertalanffy}(\text{age}_i, \\
                & L_{ m/f }, A_{ m/f }, K_{ m/f }) \\
    L_{ m/f } &\sim \normal{\text{population L}}{0.5} \\
    A_{ m/f } &\sim \normal{\text{population A}}{0.025} \\
    K_{ m/f } &\sim \normal{\text{population K}}{0.05} \\
    \sigma_{m/f} &\sim \normal{0.25}{0.05} \\
    \end{align*}
  \end{minipage}
      \makebox[0.9\colwidth]{$L$: asymptotic length; $A$: related to size at hatching; $K$: related to growth rate.}
    \item Bayesian model with wide priors centred around the population-level curve values was used to recover the values \parencite{rCore, rstan}.

  \end{itemize}

\block[bodyinnersep=0mm]{Conclusion}{
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Method is effective for actual dinosaur data, even at low sample sizes ($n=8$ for \tyran{})
    \item Strong evidence for \maia{} (35\% -- 62\%), weak evidence for \psit{} (-2\% -- 31\%), and moderate evidence against \tyran{} (-7\% -- 13\%) dimorphism (95\% credible intervals).
    \item Benefits: incorporation of uncertainty (calibrate the strength of our findings) and prior knowledge (constrain curve to be biologically feasible)
  \end{itemize}
}
\block[bodyinnersep=0mm]{Next Steps}{
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Incorporate measurement uncertainty (age, size) into the model
    \item \textbf{Improve sex prediction (clustering algorithms, incorporate additional features)}
    \item Use of mixture models to avoid assuming dimorphism
  \end{itemize}
